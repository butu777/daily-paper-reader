Abstract. Localization microscopy has overcome the di !raction limit, 

i.e . the conventional resolution limit of a microscope, enabling nanoscale biological imaging by precisely determining the positions of individual emitters such as single fluorescent molecules. However, the performance of deep learning methods, commonly applied to these tasks, depends sig-nificantly on the quality of training data, typically generated through simulation. Creating simulations that perfectly replicate experimental conditions remains challenging, resulting in a persistent simulation-to-experiment gap. To bridge this gap, we propose a physics-informed gen-erative model leveraging self-supervised learning directly on experimen-tal data. Our model extends the Deep Latent Particles (DLP) framework by incorporating a physical model of the Point Spread Function (PSF; the image of a single point source in the microscope) into the decoder, enabling it to disentangle learned realistic environments from emitters. Trained directly on unlabeled experimental images, our model intrinsi-cally captures realistic background, noise patterns, and emitter charac-teristics. The decoder thus acts as a high-fidelity generator, producing fully labeled, realistic training images with known emitter locations. Us-ing these generated datasets significantly improves the performance of su-pervised localization networks, particularly in challenging scenarios such as complex backgrounds and low signal-to-noise ratios. We demonstrate our approach on a variety of experimentally measured microscopy data, including super-resolution imaging in 2D and 3D and particle tracking in live cells, showing substantial improvements in localization precision and emitter detection. The code will be made publicly available.

# 1 Introduction 

Determining the precise positions of single molecules or particles in optical mi-croscopy images is a fundamental capability enabling insights at the nanoscale, with applications ranging from biology to materials science. Single-molecule lo-calization microscopy (SMLM) techniques, including PALM [5], STORM [33], and PAINT [39], use sequential localization of emitters to reconstruct images that overcome the di !raction limit, allowing detailed biological visualization [37]. Single-Particle Tracking (SPT) utilizes localization over time to determine the trajectories of moving molecules or nanoparticles, revealing information about their dynamics, interactions, and microenvironments [43]. Extending localization microscopy to three dimensions (3D) typically requires physical modification of the microscope; a common approach involves Point Spread Function (PSF) en-gineering, where optical elements modify the PSF to encode depth [15,31,40,42] or other information, such as spectral properties [41, 44] or molecular orienta-tion [2, 16, 17]. Deep learning methods have become essential tools in microscopy and SMLM, enabling powerful analysis and reconstruction [22]. They have been applied to various challenges, including the localization of dense emitters [26, 45], the clas-sification of emitter color [13], the determination of molecular orientation [19, 47, 48], and the facilitation of end-to-end optimization of imaging systems, e.g .designing the PSF together with the reconstruction algorithm [9, 13, 25, 26]. Im-portantly, the performance of many deep learning approaches, particularly super-vised methods such as DeepSTORM3D [26], depends on the quality and quantity of the training data; such training data is often generated via simulation, which relies on modeling the PSF and various other experimental factors, because ac-quiring large, accurately annotated experimental datasets for 3D localization microscopy is practically impossible. However, building simulations that perfectly mimic experimental conditions is challenging [34]. Accurately capturing complex, spatially varying background, realistic noise statistics, and system-specific aberrations requires significant ef-forts and in many cases is impractical. This leads to a persistent simulation-to-experiment (sim2exp) gap, where models trained on synthetic data underperform when applied to real measurements. Furthermore, even when an adequate sim-ulation model exists, modifying it to reflect new experimental conditions can require time-consuming manual tuning or substantial redevelopment e !orts. In this work, we aim to bridge the sim2exp gap by introducing a physics-informed self-supervised generative framework that learns directly from experi-mental data. We propose PILPEL – Physics-Informed Latent Particles for Emit-ter Localization , an object-centric self-supervised generative model built on the Deep Latent Particles (DLP [6, 7]) framework. We extend DLP by incorporat-ing an explicit physical PSF model into the decoder that takes encoded spatial coordinates as input. Trained directly on unlabeled experimental images, this ar-chitecture allows the model to intrinsically learn realistic background and noise patterns while disentangling the learned environment from the precise placement and properties of the emitters.

The trained PILPEL model thus serves as a data generator, producing real-istic, fully-labeled training images with known ground truth emitter locations. This generated data, embodying experimentally learned characteristics, serves as highly realistic training material for supervised localization algorithms. Using these generated datasets significantly improves the performance of supervised localization networks on real-world data, particularly in challenging scenarios such as complex backgrounds and low signal-to-noise ratios, enabling their use across diverse experimental conditions, and improving their ease of application. Our contributions are summarized as follows: (1) we propose a physics-informed Deep Latent Particle model, a novel self-supervised object-centric gen-erative image representation model that builds on DLP and is used to generate realistic, labeled training data embodying experimentally learned characteristics that can be used to train supervised localization models such as DeepSTORM3D; (2) we demonstrate that the use of the generated data leads to superior perfor-mance in downstream localization tasks, showing significant improvements in localization accuracy and detection rates, particularly on challenging test data with complex backgrounds and low signal-to-noise ratios; and (3) we highlight the generalizability and applicability advantage of automatically learning data characteristics directly from measurements, bridging the sim2exp gap without manual tuning of simulation parameters, or explicit modeling of experimental attributes, thereby unlocking the application of supervised localization networks to novel biological domains. 

# 2 Related Work 

Deep learning for microscopy. Recent large vision foundation models, in-cluding adaptations for microscopy [1, 11, 24], show promise for tasks such as segmentation, cellular mapping or classification, but their direct application to generating high-precision localization microscopy data is challenging. These models are often pre-trained on di !erent data domains ( e.g ., natural images or other microscopy modalities) and are typically designed for tasks like segmenta-tion rather than sub-pixel localization. Additionally, fine-tuning or training such large models specifically for localization microscopy is hindered by the lack of extensive labeled datasets, particularly for diverse experimental conditions and signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), or involving engineered PSFs where computationally designed optics alter image characteristics. Concurrently, VAE models have been shown to have the ability to encode emitters’ z-position in their latent space [46]. Our work contributes to this area by introducing a physics-informed VAE-based model, designed to generate realistic, label-consistent localization-microscopy images. 

Generative models for SMLM. In the domain of data synthesis, the challenge of generating realistic training data for deep learning in localization microscopy remains critical due to the di "culty in accurately simulating com-plex experimental conditions. However, the generated data is typically unlabeled, and thus can not be used for training localization models. Other generative approaches, such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [4, 10, 18, 49], also attempt to bridge the sim2exp gap by learning mappings from simulated to real domains or generating images from labels. However, such post-hoc image transformations are fundamentally incompatible with the sub-pixel precision demands of SMLM. Any pixel-level modification of a simulated image, however subtle, can deform the PSF shape or shift its apparent center, breaking the correspondence between the image content and the original ground-truth emitter coordinates. At nanoscale resolution, where localization errors on the order of tens of nanometers are meaningful, there is no tolerance for label cor-ruption introduced by an unconstrained generative process. Without an explicit physical model of image formation embedded within the generator itself, there is no mechanism to guarantee that the ground-truth labels remain valid after the image is transformed. This is precisely the design principle behind PILPEL: by placing the PSF model inside the decoder, emitter coordinates are not labels imposed on a generated image - they are the direct cause of it, ensuring exact label integrity by construction. Since, to the best of our knowledge, no other generative baseline outputs 3D sub-pixel coordinates, we compare our generated data against that of the DeepSTORM3D [26] physics-based simulator.

These simulations incorporate realistic experimental variability, including di !er-ent emitter densities, signal-to-noise ratios, background levels, and noise models. A significant step in the DS3D analysis pipeline is the generation of training data that resembles the experiment. This involves significant manual tuning and can be highly time-consuming even for an experienced user. Our model, among other contributions, alleviates this manual tuning completely. In this work, we show results with variations of the Tetrapod mask, but the flexibility of the model makes it applicable to any PSF ( e.g . astigmatism [15], double-helix [31], etc.), by inserting a di !erent phase mask and accompanied optical parameters to the PSF model. Deep Latent Particles (DLP [6, 7]) is a self-supervised, object-centric image representation model that decomposes an input image into a set of la-tent “particles,” each representing a localized foreground object with learned at-tributes such as position, scale, transparency, and visual features. An additional background particle represents the background visual features. This enables a latent representation that disentangles the objects from the scene. A key com-ponent of the original DLP is its use of a spatial softmax (SSM) operator as a spatial prior to particle positions. The model’s objective is to reconstruct the original input image, which is done by decoding the latent particles and compos-ing them together with the background to form the output image. Training is performed end-to-end using a variational autoencoder (VAE [21]) framework, by maximizing the evidence lower bound (ELBO), combining image reconstruction loss with a KL-divergence regularization term. For more details, see supplemen-tary Sec. B. 

# 4 Method

4.2 Model Architecture 

Our model architecture is illustrated in Fig. 2. The input image is initially di-vided into overlapping patches. Each patch undergoes a 3D template matching process against a dictionary of pre-calculated PSFs spanning the expected axial 

z range. This yields an initial estimation of potential 3D PSF positions ( lx, l y , l z

coordinates) within each patch. The maximum cross-correlation score is used as a confidence metric to filter out patches unlikely to contain any PSFs, resulting in a proposed set of latent particles. Following this likelihood-driven proposal stage, we extract image patches cen-tered around each proposed PSF location. These patches, intended to capture both the PSF and its local surroundings, serve as input to a particle encoder mod-ule. The encoder outputs the latent attributes for each proposed object. These are disentangled by construction, with specific latent attributes representing o !-sets ( ldx , l dy , l dz ) to refine the initial position estimate, the PSF’s intensity lI ,and additional latent attributes encoding the appearance features of the patch. The intensity factor lI allows the model to capture experiment-specific SNR and brightness distributions, and enables an automatic pruning mechanism — by driving the factor to zero during optimization — which adapts to varying emitter densities by removing unnecessary proposals. The decoder reconstructs the image from these latent representations and consists of two distinct components. The first is the di !erentiable physical layer that takes the refined spatial coordinates (initial estimate and the learned o !set, 

lx + ldx , etc.) and the intensity factor from the latent vector lI to generate a PSF image. The second component is a CNN-based decoder which takes the remaining latent appearance attributes and generates a corresponding image patch representing the local background surrounding the PSF. These are com-bined and placed into the final reconstructed image at their corresponding global coordinates. To learn global background characteristics, the generated object patches are used to mask the input image. This masked input is processed by a separate encoder-decoder network dedicated to modeling and reconstructing the general

4.3 Data Generation for Supervised Learning 

The decoder of our trained PILPEL model serves as a high-fidelity generator of realistic labeled images. By sampling coordinates and latent variables from the latent space — where the newly provided coordinates to the physical layer are, by definition, the ground truth labels for the generated image — the decoder produces images with known emitter locations and intensities. Most importantly and key to our application: these generated, labeled images inherit the realistic background and noise characteristics learned from the experimental data, as well as emitter densities, global structures and spatial distribution (see Supplemen-tary Fig. S5). This capability allows for the generation of large and accurately labeled training datasets for supervised localization models, specifically DS3D [26] in this work. This data closely mimic real measurements, thereby narrowing the sim2exp gap while automatically capturing unmodeled experimental nuances.

We first establish the method’s accuracy on controlled simulations with known ground truth, then demonstrate its impact on real biological data – three datasets of 3D localiza-tion and one dataset of 2D localization. 5.2 Super-resolution 3D Imaging 

Having validated our approach under controlled conditions with known ground truth, we next evaluate its performance on complex, real-world biological data. Specifically, we apply the method to a 3D super-resolution dataset of mitochon-dria [29] imaged via SMLM (see Supplementary Sec. E for experimental details). As shown in Fig. 4, training on our generated data dramatically improves the final reconstruction. The number of detected localizations increased from 119 Kto 581 K ( → 4.9↑ increase) across 20 K frames. This factor, namely, the number of localizations, is crucial in SMLM, and translates to improved image quality, i.e .fuller features (as seen in Fig. 4b), or faster acquisition. Fig. 4c shows measured frames and corresponding PILPEL reconstructions. It is possible that this significant increase in detections also includes a higher rate of false positives; however, this can be addressed using standard density filters commonly applied in SMLM reconstruction [30]. For the visualization in Fig. 4, the reconstruction from our method was post-processed with such a filter to match the original’s noise level, while the original reconstruction was left as-is, as filtering would degrade the sparse biological structure. # 6 Limitations and Future Work 

Despite the advances demonstrated, our method exhibits several limitations. Training the generative model requires additional computational time compared to training on synthetic datasets, although it significantly reduces manual hu-man intervention (see Supplementary Material for computational details). Ad-ditionally, accurate reconstruction still necessitates a reliable prior PSF model, obtainable by a numerical model or from experimental calibration followed by phase retrieval. Moreover, the e !ectiveness of our approach depends on the recon-struction quality and its resemblance to actual measurements. If reconstruction quality is insu "cient, adjusting parameters such as patch size or the number of latent particles may be required. Future work includes leveraging this generative capability for complex-to-simulate microscopy scenarios (e.g., particle flows, living organisms like C. el-egans), extending to dynamic tracking via DDLP for video [7], and exploring latent space manipulation for tailored dataset generation for downstream tasks (e.g., denoising). Another promising direction is to incorporate learned aberra-tions [23], enabling also the optimization of the PSF model itself. 

# 7 Conclusions 

We introduced a physics-informed generative model that bridges the critical sim2exp gap in localization microscopy. By integrating an explicit PSF model within a self-supervised, object-centric framework, the model can disentangle the emitter properties from complex background and noise characteristics. Trained on real experimental measurements, our approach generates high-fidelity labeled training data that captures the nuances of real measurements. As our bench-marks demonstrate, training supervised models on this generated data signif-icantly enhances localization accuracy and detection rates, especially for chal-lenging or novel experimental conditions. This approach eliminates the need for manual simulation tuning and the explicit modeling of complex image elements, o!ering a robust, adaptable solution for improving deep learning performance in microscopy.
