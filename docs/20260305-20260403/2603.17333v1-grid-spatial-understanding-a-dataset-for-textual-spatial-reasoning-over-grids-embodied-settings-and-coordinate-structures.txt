# Grid Spatial Understanding: A Dataset for Textual Spatial Reasoning over Grids, Embodied Settings, and Coordinate Structures 

Risham Sidhu, Julia Hockenmaier 

University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign 

Abstract 

We introduce GSU, a text-only grid dataset to evaluate the spatial reasoning capabilities of LLMs over 3 core tasks: navigation, object localization, and structure composition. By for-going visual inputs, isolating spatial reasoning from perception, we show that while most mod-els grasp basic grid concepts, they struggle with frames of reference relative to an embodied agent and identifying 3D shapes from coordi-nate lists. We also find that exposure to a visual modality does not provide a generalizable un-derstanding of 3D space that VLMs are able to utilize for these tasks. Finally, we show that while the very latest frontier models can solve the provided tasks (though harder variants may still stump them), fully fine-tuning a small LM or LORA fine-tuning a small LLM show po-tential to match frontier model performance, suggesting an avenue for specialized embodied agents. 

1 Introduction 

With the advent of more powerful LLMs, their ap-plications have broaden from text generation to complex problems ranging from advanced mathe-matical reasoning to multimodal agents. Many of these tasks require the ability to reason about space, whether to solve geometry problems (Zhao et al., 2025; Gao et al., 2025) or to understand verbal commands about their surroundings as an embod-ied agent (Zhang et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2025), to complete tasks while interacting with objects in complex environments (Padmakumar et al., 2021; Shridhar et al., 2020), to describe visual scenes (Fu et al., 2024a), or to reason over 2- and 3-D images (Cheng et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). Approaches to such tasks incorporate additional modalities through vision encoders, projections of point cloud data, or more specialized representa-tions, but using these effectively necessitates up-dates to base LLM architecture or additional train-ing. Furthermore, even with such additions, it is unclear if fundamentally non-physical models that have been trained primarily on unpaired text can truly understand 3D space. Despite recent advances on various spatial reasoning tasks, the internal rea-soning processes of LLMs, VLMs, and variations thereof are often not interpretable, meaning that merely measuring performance is distinct from evaluating understanding. And even when perfor-mance is strong, further investigations into how additional modalities, like vision, are used in spa-tial tasks reveal that multi-modal LLMs receive little additional information from images when pro-vided with rich text representations (Wang et al., 2024a). In that case, textual descriptions of physical space may serve as a convenient proxy for complex visual inputs when evaluating spatial reasoning. By removing visual perceptions, they offer a controlled method of probing whether LLMs posses robust internal representations of space, and if such repre-sentations can be manipulated with textual descrip-tions alone. Given that grids are an established rep-resentation for 3D space, they are a natural choice to express 3D layouts and movements. In this work, we present GSU, a dataset com-posed of 3 simple text-only grid spatial reasoning tasks aimed at applications in embodied domains, to serve as a diagnostic for the inherent spatial reasoning abilities of existing models and identify where further work is needed to induce the spacial capabilities embodied agents require. • The Navigation Task requires instruction gen-eration or following on 2D and 3D grids with two potential spatial frames of reference: Car-dinal (i.e. fixed), testing recognition of 1D di-rectional differences in coordiantes, and Ego-centric (i.e. moving with an embodied agent), testing understanding of rotating/updating spa-tial reference frames. • The Object Localization task asks where tar-

2 The GSU Dataset 

We now expand on each of these relatively sim-ple spatial tasks and the specific facets of textual coordinate reasoning they test. For more details on generation and dataset statistics, please refer to Appendix A. 

2.1 The Navigation Task 

The first task measures models’ understanding of textual coordinates as a representation of physical space. In this task, models must correlate natural language directions (i.e. "left", "down", "back-ward") with grid traversals across coordinates dif-fering in 1 dimension at a time, i.e. equating mov-ing from (0, 0) to (2, 0), to (2, 1) with moving "right 2" and "forward 1". 

2.1.1 Instructor vs. Follower 

VLN tasks are generally a conversation between two counterparts: an instructor and a follower. While the instructor is often a person and the fol-lower an agent, this is not necessarily always the case (e.g. a collaborative system with multiple agents, Narayan-Chen et al. (2019)’s MDC Archi-tect task, voice assistants, etc). Models should thus be proficient in both directions of this mapping between verbal spatial directions and coordinate traversals. In the Follower setting, models receive a set of natural language instructions explaining how to tra-verse the grid (e.g. "move right 2 steps and then forward 1" ) and must output a coordinate reflecting their final location (e.g. (2 , 1) ). Whereas in the 

Instructor setting, models receive a grid traversal expressed as a series of intermediate coordinates (e.g. (0 , 0) , (2 , 0) , (2 , 1) ) and must output the natu-ral language instructions that correlate to that path (e.g. "move right 2 and forward 1" ). 

2.1.2 Spatial Frames of Reference 

We consider two frames of reference for the spatial directions of the grid. In the Cardinal setting, the spatial directions are fixed to the axes of the grid and never change, i.e. to move forward always means to increase the y value, while to move left always means to decrease the x value. While many grid based tasks use this fixed perspective of spa-tial relations (e.g. Aghzal et al. (2025)), it does not transfer well to practical collaborative settings where an embodied agent or a human it is instruct-ing have their own frame of reference that may not align with a fixed grid. The Egocentric formulation aligns spatial direc-tions with the perspective of an embodied subject navigating the grid, orienting the spatial directions with their body, and so rotating where they are rel-ative to the fixed axes of the grid. For example, in the Egocentric setting to go right 2 steps and then forward 1 step would first entail turning to the right (90 ◦ clockwise to face +X) and moving two steps in that direction. Then, moving 1 step forward would no longer mean increasing the y

value, but rather continuing forward in our current heading/direction of travel, i.e. increasing our x

value. This would be equivalent to moving 2 steps right and then 1 more step right in the Cardinal 

setting. (See Figure 1 for a visualization.) Additionally, we include a simpler form of the Egocentric Instructor setting, Card2Ego .Rather than going from coordinates to ego-centric spatial directions, Card2Ego asks models to translate cardinal spatial directions (North/South/East/West) into egocentric spatial directions (left/right/forward/backward).

2.3 The Structure Composition Task 

The final task explores a model’s ability to under-stand how blocks/voxels can be composed into larger overall structures. Not only does it require an understanding of how coordinates relate to real space, but also a knowledge of basic shapes/forms (e.g. a series of blocks that differ only in their z-dimension are stacked vertically and thus a column) and the ability to compose large lists of coordinates into meaningful information. 

2.3.1 Composition Types 

In the most basic setting, Simple , the selected co-ordinates form one structure of a single color and the model must interpret what 3D shape their ar-rangement forms (e.g. "an orange row 5 blocks long" or "a 3 x 3 x 3 green cube" ). Slightly more complicated is the Cohesive setting where multiple colors of blocks form a single structure and the challenge is to understand how those colors inter-act with the structure (e.g. compare "a row with alternating orange and blue blocks" and "a row with an orange left half and a blue right half" ). Fi-nally, the Composite setting places 3 single-colored shapes adjacent to each other, asking the model to identify the 3 shapes, their colors, and where they are relative to each other. 

3 Experiments 

To explore grid spatial understanding over a variety of model families, sizes, and types, we tested 5 broad categories of models. First, we tested small text-only models: Mistral-7b (Jiang et al. (2023)’s Mistral-7b-Instruct-v0.3), Llama-8b (Grattafiori et al. (2024)’s Llama3.1-8b-Instruct), and Qwen-7b (Yang et al. (2024); Team (2024)’s Qwen2.5-7b-Instruct). Additionally, we prompted their most similarly sized Vision-Language counterparts to see if exposure to a visual modality (even if it is not being utilized) can induce further spa-tial understanding: Pixtral-12b (Agrawal et al., 2024), LlamaVL-11b (Grattafiori et al. (2024)’s Llama3.2-11b-Vision-Instruct), and QwenVL-7b (Team (2025a); Bai et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024b)’s Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct). We also eval-uated larger models, as they are known to be more powerful than their smaller counterparts: GPT-OSS20b (OpenAI, 2025), Qwen32b and QwenVL-32b ((Team, 2025b)’s 32b Instruct variants), Olmo-32b (Olmo et al., 2025), Qwen-72b (Yang et al. (2024); Team (2024)’s Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct). Fi-nally, to consider frontier models, we choose GPT-4o (OpenAI et al., 2024) and Gemini-3-Pro (Team et al., 2025) accessed as Gemini-3.1-Pro-Preview, and to compare overall performance to a much smaller model (<1B params) that can be fully fine-tuned, we fully train FlanT5-large (Chung et al., 2022). For the purposes of prompting we generate test sets for each task of size 100 and for training we generate another of size 3000 (we did not see marked improvement in training by increasing the dataset size beyond this, see Appendix B.1 for more details). The Navigation and Object Localization 

experiments in Table 1 are 1-shot prompting with a handwritten example that includes the full reason-ing needed to generate an answer. For the Structure Composition task in Table 1 the experiments are 3-shot prompting with synthetic examples and no reasoning. These setting were chosen based on per-formance (see Tables 9 and 15 in the Appendix), though we explain the difference in behavior as: the Navigation and Object Localization tasks re-quire some amount of reasoning to work out head-ings and spatial relations, thus benefit more from one dense example that gives an example reason-ing trace, while the Structure Composition task needs no explicit reasoning (just the ability to com-pose coordinates together) and thus benefits from a larger quantity of examples. 

3.1 Metrics 3.1.1 Navigation Metrics 

For the Follower task, we evaluate the accuracy of the predicted final coordinates as an exact match (i.e. there is no partial credit for getting the x-coordinate coordinate correct if the y-coordinate in incorrect) and for the Instructor we measure the exact match accuracy of the generated instruc-tion chain (i.e. every step must be correct for the model to get credit). For a less exacting metric, we computed the distance between the true final coor-dinates and: the generated coordinates in Follower 

and the final coordinates obtained by executing the generated instruction chain in Instructor . As they tracked closely with the accuracies they are reported in see Appendix Table 8. 

3.1.2 Object Localization Metrics 

To measure model performance on the Object Lo-calization tasks, we compute, Os, the term overlap of the spatial relations in the predicted text, p, and the ground truth text, g.

Os = Er(p) ∩ Er(g)

Er(p) ∪ Er(g) ∗ 100 

where the function Er creates a set from each in-dividual spatial relation extracted from the text. Essentially, it takes how many of the spatial terms in the generated sequence p are correct and divides this by the size of the set union of all correct spa-tial terms in the ground truth, g, and all generated spatial terms in p. For example, if the correct spa-tial relations, Er(g) are {"front", "left"}, the model may score a 0 ( Er(p) = {"right"}), a 33.33 ( Er(p)

= {"front", "above"}), a 50 ( Er(p) = {"front"}), a 66.67 ( Er(p) = {"above", "front", "left"}), or a 100 (Er(p) = {"front", "left"}). This penalizes mod-els for predicting incorrect terms, while reducing this penalty for harder examples with more spatial relations to account for. 

3.1.3 Structure Composition Metrics 

We continue to use the spatial overlap metric, Os,described in 3.1.2 and add three additional overlap metrics: Oc, which measures the overlap of the color terms, On, which measures numerical over-lap, and Of , which measures the overlap of the structure formation terms. For On, we use the same simple overlap as Os, dividing how many correct numbers were generated by the size of the union of all the ground truth numbers and all generated num-bers. The numerical overlap ( On) errors are caused by models miscounting or due to unconven-tional ways of breaking up structure descriptions: e.g. rather than describing a 3 x 3 x 8 rectangular prism with those dimensions, one generation de-scribes it as a group of 10 columns each 7 blocks high. While this could describe a similar shape (though it could also be a 7 x 10 plane) and cer-tainly accounts for a similar number of blocks, it receives no credit for either metric. Overall, the generated descriptions are compre-hensible and seem reasonable when viewed inde-pendently of the ground truth. In 0-shot settings (Appendix Table 14), models generated vague an-swers, e.g.: "a continuous layer of blocks" rather than concrete shapes, e.g. "a 4 x 7 rectangle" . This behavior extends into the few-shot setting, with numerous productions of "structure", "formation", and "platform" modified by rich adjectives, such as "a large, flat, expansive structure" . Models also flatten 3D shapes, outputting "square" for cube and "rectangle" for rectangular prisms. Other times they name a shape incorrectly, but still display in-sight into the formed structure, such as calling a 2 x 2 x 8 rectangular prism a "cube with height 8 and width 2" (fundamentally not a cube, but still displaying spatial understanding that is rewarded by the On metric instead). A notable threshold that stronger models cross is distinguishing rows and columns: stronger models identify which axes the blocks differ on and rarely conflate the two, instead mixing up cubes with rectangular prisms or verti-cal rectangular prisms with columns. Generally, weaker models also omit reasoning for the shapes they produce, while stronger models like Gemini explain how the coordinate ranges influence its pro-ductions, which is reflected in its scores. 

4.1 Manipulating Spatial Reference Frames 

The reasoning traces of the models corroborate the score disparity between Cardinal and Egocentric Navigation tasks in Tables 1 and 10. In the for-mer setting, most models output neat, contained logic with only occasional errors, while in the let-ter, models fail to keep the X and Y axes oriented consistently, leading to generations such as "we are facing −Y with +Y to our right" and "we turned towards +X, so we’re facing −X". Larger models, though less likely to make these mistakes, calculate updated headings after a turn and then "forget" to use them in the next step, outputting logic and final coordinates consistent with the fixed grids of the 

Cardinal setting. In the Egocentric Object Localization tasks, this aversion to non-standard spatial reference frames manifests as non sequitur logic where the model establishes the correct heading in the premise but completes the generation with an incorrect deduc-tion. These deductions are generally consistent with the standard +Y heading, e.g. while fac-ing −Y : "I know that −X is to my right [correct premise] "so x values smaller than me are to my left" [incorrect deduction consistent with a +Y

heading instead]. The spatial term overlap, Os is also higher for the +Y heading than any other (Ap-pendix Table 12). This phenomenon might also explain poor performance on the Allocentric vari-ant in Table 1, particularly the forward/backing flipping described in Figure 2, as models struggle to reconcile how two frames of reference interact with each other. We suspect that the inability to correctly reori-ent the spatial reference frame is due in part to exposure to the standard Cartesian orientation in pre-training and in part due to the complexity of updating it. This is supported by the Table 1 Navi-gation Card2Ego results, where any mathematical reasoning about textual grid coordinates is com-pletely absent, replaced by converting Cardinal di-rections into relative ones. LLMs have an easier time with Card2Ego than the Egocentric Instruc-tor but still fall short, showing that, in addition to struggling with grid coordinates, LLMs consis-tently struggle with the concept of rotating spatial frames of reference outside of explicit grids too. 

4.2 Effects of the Visual Modality 

As shown by the 4 VLMs shown in light blue un-der their LLM counterparts in Table 1, there is no clear correlation between exposure to a visual modality and improved reasoning over textual coor-dinate grids, especially in the smaller models. For example, on the Navigation tasks, Pixtral outper-forms Mistral, while Qwen outperforms Qwen-VL, and both Llama models perform similarly, though perhaps the consistent pattern within each model family can be attributed to how vision is integrated into their respective VLMs. In some of the tasks (e.g. Object Localization ), we see that Qwen-32B-VL outperformed Qwen-32B and in others (i.e. 

Structure Composition ), all VLMs outperform their LLM counterparts: these patterns may suggest that VLMs need at least a certain LLM size before being able to utilize information gained from interacting with a textual modality or that they confer more benefits when reasoning over coordinate structures rather than spacial relations. 

4.3 Frontier Capabilities 

As expected, the frontier models outperform smaller LLMs, though notably Qwen-72b is able to match their performance on some tasks and even outperform GPT-4o. While proficient in the Cardinal setting, GPT-4o struggles to keep track of spatial references in the Egocentric one, incor-rectly computing the current heading or forgetting to act according to an updated heading, especially in longer sequences (see Appendix Table 11). For example, GPT-4o generates that it was "currently facing −X, but need[ed] to move right [and mov-ing] right means turning 90 degrees clockwise so that’s +X". This final heading should have been 

+Y , a rotation that GPT handles successfully in shorter sequences. However, a right turn leading to facing +X is consistent with the fixed Cardinal 

frame of reference, which the model seems to de-fault to, even in the midst of egocentric-compliant reasoning. The latest Gemini model solves multiple tasks completely, even harder variants of the tasks such as arbitrarily long navigation sequences with non-integer step lengths or non-axis aligned turns. It also performs surprisingly well on distinguishing independent block structures from scrambled lists of individual blocks. Given this, we must acknowl-edge that Gemini-3-Pro has spatial understanding when it comes to spatial frames of reference rela-tive to an embodied agent and composing blocks into larger structures. At the very least, it must have been exposed to similar problems, perhaps in a mathematical context. However, that is not to say there is no room for improvement within the domain of textual grid based tasks. We further prompt Gemini on a task combining each of the 3 task categories: an ego-centric agent must follow navigation instructions and then answer where two block-based structures are relative to each other (see E.6). This requires the model to: (a) successfully complete egocentric navigation and identify its final heading, (b) dis-tinguish the target and reference structures from distractor objects, all presented as a single list of blocks, and (c) identify where the target is relative to the reference. Here, we find that even Gemini fell short, with a final spatial overlap Os of 0.62: it sometimes mixes up similar shapes, struggles with the forward/backward issue, and makes slight er-rors in relative spatial relations given the 3D nature of block structures. 

4.4 Improving Small LLMs 

We anticipate that more frontier models will de-velop enough spatial reasoning to solve the base tasks, and eventually the harder variants as well. However, the simplicity of GSU and the subsequent gap between it and real-world scenarios, motivates us to want stronger small LLMs. To that end, we train our worst performing small LLM (Llama-8b) with LORA to elicit an underlying spatial under-standing. Initial experiments reduced performance on the Navigation task and so to better match natu-ral LLM outputs, we generate synthetic reasoning traces to accompany the ground truth answers for the Navigation and Object Localization tasks. (This also matched our prompting findings in Appendix Tables 9 and 15 that showed a single example with strong reasoning improved performance more than multiple examples without reasoning.) As we show in Table 1, with these reasoning traces, Llama-8b shows improved performance on the Object Local-ization tasks (the reasoning traces offered slight LORA performance boosts). It also more than dou-bles its performance on the Navigation Follower 

tasks after LORA, outperforming GPT-4o and al-most matching Gemini. For the Navigation Instruc-tor tasks, improvement is even more drastic for the 

Egocentric and Card2Ego versions it initially strug-gled with, increasing performance tenfold. With this, we show that textual spatial reasoning is not out of reach for smaller models and their potential to match frontier models. We also note that fully finetuning a small model like FlanT5 (<1B params) is sufficient to outper-form many LLMs and match performance with some frontier models, especially on sequence gen-eration tasks like Navigation Instructor (Table 1). This highlights how simple the tasks should be to solve: a lightweight model a fraction of the size is better at identifying shapes from coordinate lists of blocks, where those shapes are relative to each other, and generating instructions for grid naviga-tion. 

5 Related Works 

Spatial Reasoning Instead of text, physical infor-mation is generally conveyed to LLMs via images, either from external POVs like observing scenes from above (Li et al., 2024a) or from the perspec-tive of embodied agents for applications like VLN, and pointcloud embeddings (Hong et al., 2023). However, even after integrating additional modal-ities and achieving strong performance on many visual tasks, VLMs could not understand or rea-son about physical space as capably as humans, struggling with spatial VQA, particularly object orientations in images (Liao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023) and mental transformations like rotations (Stogiannidis et al., 2025). They fall well short of human performance localizing objects within scenes and reasoning about object dimensions (Ma-jumdar et al., 2024). Some have proposed address-ing these spatial reasoning shortcomings with train-ing datasets that align 2D images with textual tasks about the orientation of, distance between, and loca-tion of nodes Tang et al. (2025), while others have questioned what VLMs are actually perceiving in images and found that they perform just as well with rich text descriptions instead of input images (Wang et al., 2024a; Fu et al., 2024b). Combined with the difficulty of isolating spatial performance from visual perception, this motivates text-only spatial reasoning to evaluate the "mental" models LLMs have of space. Some use graphs, ex-ploring graph traversals in which LLMs hallucinate nodes, take suboptimal paths, and deteriorate on denser graphs (Momennejad et al., 2023) or describ-ing spatial relations between graph nodes using multihop reasoning over intermediary nodes (Shi et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024b). Others are nominally set in grids, such as Yamada et al. (2024) which ex-plores 2D grid navigation but only on closed loop paths using relative descriptions of positions and Mayer et al. (2025), which proposes a visual puzzle game on grid, but compares visual representation to a chess-style notation for the textual representa-tion. Few consider coordinates for text-only spatial reasoning tasks (a notable exception being Aghzal et al. (2025)’s evaluation of LLM path planning through obstacles on fixed 2D grids) despite coor-dinate grids being a standard for representing space and likely plentiful in LLM pretraining data. One context in which LLMs may have encoun-tered coordinates or reasoning about them is math problems. While most coordinates in such prob-lems are paired with text and the associated rea-soning is unrelated to spatial relations, the success in mathematical reasoning (Shao et al., 2024; Ahn et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025) and a variety of math datasets (Hendrycks et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022) suggests that coordinate spatial reasoning is not outside the realm of LLM abilities. 

Applications GSU distills the spatial reasoning required in embodied settings to its simplest form. As such, the main applications of this work are in embodied AI, from VLN (Thomason et al., 2019) to cooperative task completion (Yang et al., 2025). While there are several intermediate steps before we can extend our findings to multimodal mod-els, virtual tasks may benefit more directly. Take the MDC (Narayan-Chen et al., 2019), in which two agents collaborate to build a block-based struc-ture in a 3D grid. Existing approaches struggle with shifting spatial reference frames caused by agents moving around the environment during the course of building (and thus might benefit from methods that improve performance on our Egocen-tric Navigation task). The Object Localization task addresses the issue of generating spatial relations for blocks relative to existing structures while the 

Structure Composition aligns with the MDC Ar-chitect describing a target structure, a part of the task that remains largely unexplored as recent work has focused on the instruction-following Builder (Jayannavar et al., 2025). Our focus on smaller or unimodal models is further motivated by the practical constraints of real-world embodied AI, where the latencies of frontier models can significantly hinder a physi-cal agent (Park et al., 2026; Pollini et al., 2025), hardware constraints may require lightweight mod-els that can be fully housed inside a small robot, privacy concerns may necessitate local models or eschewing multimodal inputs at times (Marchang and Di Nuovo, 2022; Moncrieff et al., 2008), and specialized tasks may need further training anyway. 

6 Conclusion 

In this work, we propose GSU, a novel evaluation framework providing a controlled environment to test spatial reasoning and spatial communication without confounding factors like visual perception. Evaluating a variety of models, we found that most, even some frontier models, struggle to track rotat-ing frames of spatial reference and understand how blocks at grid coordinates may be composed into structures, though recent models are crossing this threshold of spatial understanding. We also provide empirical evidence that exposure to a visual modal-ity does not inherently guarantee improved spatial reasoning, or at least, communication about it, es-pecially in smaller models, which goes against the current assumptions about VLM capabilities and raises questions about how the parametric knowl-edge gained from the modality is stored and ac-cessed by models. Finally, we show that training lightweight models and small LLMs can match or outperform massive foundation models on these textual grid spatial reasoning tasks, supporting their use as specialized embodied agents. Limitations 

This work is, in part, limited by the nature of the proposed tasks. While the simplicity allows for ex-tremely controlled observations on understanding, it is noted that such tasks may not directly gener-alize to real world settings outside of virtual envi-ronments. In particular, embodied scenarios that involve continuous space, visual noise, and partial observability, are not easily represented in the cur-rent formulation of GSU. That being said, we think generalizing to continuous space on a grid should be quite feasible, as math is not generally the fea-ture that models struggle the most with. However, having more angles of rotation available to models in Navigation tasks will certainly increase the dif-ficulty movement would no longer be nicely axes aligned (though arguable with the egocentric set-ting the most challenging component is keeping track of headings). In the future, it would be inter-esting to expand textual problems to incorporate partial observability, exploring if models can tell what would be in their line of sight and reason-ing about what may be obscured. It might also present a means of checking information stored during exploration, creating more interesting prob-lems such as reconciling information gathered from different viewpoints (that may not be expressed rel-ative to the same overarching fixed grid). The most direction extension of grid coordinates into more physical problems would likely be in pointclouds and we would be interested to see if our findings generalize to those richer, longer contexts. Within the confines of the task, we think there is room to further improve smaller models on GSU, explore possibilities such as using performance on a Follower task with more direct signal to improve 

Instructor performance, and see if forcing models to communicate about and reason over textual grids can force stronger internal representations or help connect parametric knowledge to visual inforam-tion in multimodal models.

A.0.3 Structure Composition 

To generate the Structure Composition task, we first generate 3 shapes and associated verbal de-scriptions using a template with minor variations for synonyms and grammatical structure. In the 

Simple setting we select the first shape and associ-ated description, whereas in the Cohesive setting we then adjust half the blocks in the structure to be a different color and update the verbal description accordingly. Finally, for the Composite setting we translate the 3 shapes to be adjacent and join their textual descriptions with the appropriate spatial terms. We initially explored a variety of textual repre-sentations for coordinates. In our Plain representa-tion, coordinates are input into LLMs as sequences resembling pseudo-code: 

COLOR 1 X1 Y1 Z1

... 

COLOR N XN YN ZN

We also explore alternate representations of co-ordinates, which this task is particularly suited for since it requires processing far more coordinates than previous tasks. The Set representation closely resembles Plain , but utilizes structures that the model might have encountered in code: "( COLOR 1, X1, Y1, Z1), ..., ( COLOR N , XN ,

YN , ZN )" 

Dict resembles a dictionary where each value is explicitly labeled: "(color = COLOR 1, x = X1, y = Y1, z = Z1), ..., (color = COLOR N , x = XN , y = YN , z = ZN )" 

Text is a more verbal representation and the one used in the previous tasks: "a COLOR 1 block at ( X1, Y1, Z1), ..., and a 

COLOR N block at ( XN , YN , ZN )". However, for brevity, we chose the overall best performing representation for the main paper, and include the supplementary results here (see 4. We found it interesting that, in general, the Set and Text 

representations were the best performing across all models (with notable exceptions, such as Qwen family models highly preferring the Dict represen-tation), despite the convention in prior works to use the Plain formulation. We also anticipated the 

Set representation performing most similarly to the 

Plain as they were the most similar and were sur-prised to see that performance was quite different across these two settings. While the Text repre-sentation is not the most efficient, it follows that textual models would be able to best parse informa-tion presented in a form closer to natural language than to a set or dictionary. A.1 Shape Metric 

For the shape metric, we check for synonyms in the generation and map them to their correspond-ing shape. The synonyms are built around the base vocabulary and then appended to with common out-puts in actual generations. For columns, we accept "column", as well any production with "vertical" or "upright" and "line" or "row". For rows, we accept any generation with "row" or "line" that does not mention verticality. For towers (multiple columns grouped together), we accept "tower", "rectangu-lar prism", and "pillar". For planes (rectangular prisms only one block thick), we accept "plane", "platform", "rectangle", "wall", "square", "ring", and "O". For cubes, we accept "cube". Furthermore, since shapes can be similar to oth-ers, if a generation does not match at least one of the ground truth shapes, we offer it partial credit based on how similar the shape it predicted was. Confusing rows and columns leads 0.6 of the full 1.0 received for a total match, as does mixing up a column and a tower. Since towers and cubes are both rectangular prisms, we offer half credit for such mistakes, and 0.1 for towers and planes or planes and cubes, and 0.2 for rows and towers. 

B Experimental Information 

B.1 Training Details 

For the LORA fine-tuning runs, we experimented with various ranks, learning rates, and training set sizes. Overall, we found that the model perfor-mance plateaued at its highest level with rank 64, a learning rate between 5e-4 and 1e-5 depending on the task, and a training set of size 3000. We used alpha=8 as recommended in the original paper and an AdamW optimizer. We allowed the models to train for 4 epochs, but chose the model with the highest validation accuracy as the one to evaluate. For the FlanT5-large and BART-base runs, we al-low the model to train for 15 epochs or until the loss converges using an AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 5e-5 for Flan and 1e-4 for BART. This project was run on up to 4 gpuA100x4’s using approximately 1500 GPU hours of compute.
