Abstract. We present FlowIt, a novel architecture for optical flow esti-mation designed to robustly handle large pixel displacements. At its core, FlowIt leverages a hierarchical transformer architecture that captures ex-tensive global context, enabling the model to effectively model long-range correspondences. To overcome the limitations of localized matching, we formulate the flow initialization as an optimal transport problem. This formulation yields a highly robust initial flow field, alongside explicitly derived occlusion and confidence maps. These cues are then seamlessly integrated into a guided refinement stage, where the network actively propagates reliable motion estimates from high-confidence regions into ambiguous, low-confidence areas. Extensive experiments across the Sin-tel, KITTI, Spring, and LayeredFlow datasets validate the efficacy of our approach. FlowIt achieves state-of-the-art results on the compet-itive Sintel and KITTI benchmarks, while simultaneously establishing new state-of-the-art cross-dataset zero-shot generalization performance on Sintel, Spring, and LayeredFlow. 

Keywords: Optical Flow · Motion Estimation · Dense Correspondence 

## 1 Introduction 

Optical flow estimation, the task of determining the 2D motion of pixels between consecutive frames, is a cornerstone of dynamic scene understanding, crucial for many downstream applications such as video action recognition [51, 66, 82], autonomous driving [14, 25, 45], frame interpolation [18, 39, 78, 82], and video

in-painting [13, 32, 77]. In recent years, the landscape of deep learning-based optical flow has been heavily dominated by iterative local search architectures [28,48,67,71]. While these recurrent refinement models achieve impressive scores on constrained benchmarks by iteratively updating a flow field using local corre-lation lookups, their fundamental reliance on local search mechanisms inherently limits their ability to enforce long-range global consistency. This limitation of-ten leads to sub-optimal generalization, particularly in scenarios involving large displacements, severe occlusions, or textureless regions. Conversely, global matching architectures [27, 74, 81] provide a theoretically more robust alternative by explicitly capturing long-range dependencies and utilizing comprehensive context to resolve local ambiguities. However, the pro-hibitive computational and memory costs associated with dense 4D correlation volumes restrict these models to heavily downsampled feature maps, often at  

> 18

of the original resolution. Compounding this issue, the subsequent iterative refinement modules typically rely on localized search, leaving them susceptible to local minima and limiting their ability to correct large-displacement errors. In response to these challenges, we propose FlowIt , a new global match-ing network for optical flow estimation. The backbone of our framework relies on a hierarchical transformer design that facilitates rich context sharing, both horizontally within individual resolution stages and vertically across multiple scales. This feature exchange ensures that the model retains global coherence and effectively maps distant correspondences, critically enabling operations at a higher spatial scale. Drawing inspiration from recent advances in stereo match-ing that demonstrate the efficacy of Optimal Transport for disparity initializa-tion [46, 47], we formulate our initial flow extraction from the cost volume as an Optimal Transport problem [9], which yields a highly stable global flow prior for subsequent refinement. Beyond architectural enhancements, we introduce a confidence-aware refine-ment module that jointly estimates occlusion masks and confidence maps. These auxiliary predictions are leveraged to actively steer the refinement process, ex-plicitly encouraging reliable motion cues to propagate from regions of high cer-tainty into ambiguous, low-confidence zones. Supervised by a dedicated loss for-mulation, this guided propagation significantly improves the accuracy of the predicted flow field, particularly in visually challenging areas. We demonstrate the critical advantage of this approach on challenging scenes, where our model exhibits robust performance and substantial accuracy gains even in the presence of challenging visual phenomena such as transparent and reflective surfaces [73]. We comprehensively validate the performance of FlowIt across standard op-tical flow datasets, namely Sintel [7], KITTI [14], Spring [44], and Layered-Flow [73]. Our model achieves state-of-the-art results on established benchmarks, while also demonstrating very competitive zero-shot generalization capabilities. In summary, our contributions are as follows: 

– We introduce FlowIt, a novel transformer-based global matching architecture for optical flow estimation that robustly resolves large pixel displacements. FlowIt 3

To accommodate diverse computational constraints, we offer multiple model variants that optimize the trade-off between parameter size and performance. 

– We propose a specialized loss formulation that significantly enhances flow ac-curacy. Coupled with the joint estimation of confidence and occlusion maps, this approach guarantees the high reliability of the final motion predictions. 

– We establish new state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization capabilities across the Sintel, Spring, and LayeredFlow datasets, while simultaneously demon-strating superior performance on the Sintel and KITTI benchmarks. 

## 2 Related Work 

The evolution of optical flow estimation has transitioned from traditional energy minimization frameworks [5,16,80] to the current dominance of deep neural net-works. Among classical approaches, some introduced the coarse-to-fine spatial pyramid [2] to address large displacements where gradient-based optimization typically fails, which estimates motion across multiple resolutions. This is cou-pled with a warping mechanism [6] that iteratively aligns the source image to the target to resolve residual motion. Additionally, the use of discrete cost volumes storing matching costs across a search window provided robust global optimiza-tion, often enhanced by high-dimensional feature descriptors [4, 36]. These design choices now form the structural blueprint for modern deep learn-ing architectures. The shift to end-to-end deep models started with FlowNet [12], introducing a correlation layer used to compute a cost volume, followed by FlowNet 2 [22], which added multiple refinement stages making use of warp-ing. Similarly, another pillar of traditional methods, i.e., coarse-to-fine spatial pyramid, has been adopted by SpyNet [53], while PWC-Net [63] combined spa-tial pyramid with warping and cost volumes. Further advancements still built on these three pillars to optimize volumetric efficiency [79], achieve lightweight footprints [19–21], while also progressively improving the training strategies to attain optimal performance from existing architectures [64]. In the twenties, a paradigm shift in network design occurred with RAFT [67], casting flow estimation into an optimization-inspired iterative process guided by an all-pairs correlation volume, followed by further works [61] that assessed the separate contributions of architecture and training. This approach has become the cornerstone for subsequent architectures [10, 49], some of which focused on high-resolution processing [23, 24, 83], or on simplifying the iterative process to reduce complexity, at first with SEA-RAFT [71] running fewer iterations and, lately, with WAFT [70] by replacing the need for a cost volume with a warping-based iterative refinement stage. On a parallel track, the increasing popularity of vision transformers led to the development of transformer-based architectures for optical flow, such as Flow-Former [17], FlowFormer++ [58], CRAFT [60], GMFlowNet [81], and efficient high-resolution approaches [34] leveraging various forms of attention for global context. ## 3 Method

Our approach is structured into four sequential stages, as illustrated in Fig. 2. In the following, we describe each stage in detail.

Recent state-of-the-art methods [70] employ large-scale Vision Transformers for feature extraction. 3.3 Refinement 

The refinement stage consists of two key steps: a global flow correction followed by iterative local refinements. The global step targets low-confidence regions, such as occluded areas, by propagating motion information from nearby high-confidence pixels. Formally, given the initial flow field F0 and its confidence map Γ0, the global refinement produces an updated flow field [46]: 

F′

exceeds a threshold, and 0 otherwise. This allows the model to preserve trusted initial estimates while reconstructing the motion in low-confidence regions via the aggregation function. We set the confidence threshold to 0.2 in our experiments. Following a single step of global refinement, the flow field undergoes itera-tive local refinements, similar to RAFT [67], to further sharpen the estimates. During each iteration t, a refinement network predicts residual updates for the optical flow ∆Ft, the confidence map ∆Γt, and the occlusion mask ∆Ot. Up to this point, all predicted flow, occlusion, and confidence maps are com-puted at 14 resolution. Following prior work [47, 67], we employ convex upsam-pling to obtain full-resolution predictions. Specifically, each high-resolution flow vector is expressed as a convex combination of its corresponding 3 × 3 neighbor-hood in the coarse-resolution grid, where the combination weights are learned feature maps. The same upsampling strategy is applied to the occlusion and confidence maps.

## 4 Experiments 

We evaluate FlowIt on Sintel [7], KITTI [14], and Spring [44]. Following prior work [70, 71], TartanAir [69], FlyingChairs [12], and FlyingThings [43] are also used for training. 

4.1 Architecture Details 

Input images are first encoded using a CNN backbone, and multi-scale features are extracted via a U-Net–style feature pyramid network (FPN). These pyra-mid features are processed by a series of multi-resolution transformer blocks. By varying the number of transformer blocks and the channel dimensions in the encoder, we define different model variants: (S) with 128 channels and 1 transformer block, (M) with 192 channels and 2 blocks, (L) with 256 channels and 3 blocks, and (XL) with 384 channels and 3 blocks. Both the global and local refinement modules employ U-Net–style networks to further process fea-tures. The global refinement predicts adjustments via a final convolutional layer, whereas the local refiner applies a convolutional GRU, similar to RAFT [67], to iteratively update flow, confidence, and occlusion maps. 4.2 Implementation Details 

FlowIt is implemented in PyTorch [50] and trained with the AdamW opti-mizer [40] using a 1cycle learning rate scheduler [59], peaking at 10 −5, and with gradients clipped to [−1, 1] . All experiments perform three local refinement iter-ations during both training and inference. A key advantage of our architecture is its compatibility with the pretrained weights from [47]; while the tasks are different and the architectures are not identical, we can initialize similar layers using their pretrained weights. As shown in Sec. 4.5, this initialization accelerates convergence and significantly improves overall performance. 

Training Schedule. We train FlowIt on four NVIDIA H100/A100 GPUs with 64GB memory each. Our training schedule follows SEA-RAFT [71]. We first pretrain FlowIt on TartanAir [69] for 300K iterations at a resolution of 480 ×640 ,using a batch size of 8 for the (S) model and 4 for the larger variants. We then train on FlyingChairs [12] at 352 × 480 resolution, with batch sizes of 16 for the (S) and (M) models and 8 for the (L) and (XL) models. 4.5 Ablation Study 

We analyze the impact of our key design choices for FlowIt. All variants are trained on FlyingChairs [12] and evaluated on its validation split. Results are summarized in Table 5. 

Model Size. By varying the feature channel dimensions and the number of transformer blocks (see Section 4.1), we define four model variants. Although (S) and (M) variants perform similarly, we observe significant improvements with larger variants (L) and (XL). 

Loss Function. We evaluate the contribution of our loss formulation by com-paring it to a baseline that uses exponentially increasing weights, similar to RAFT [67]. In that baseline, confidence and occlusion maps are not directly supervised, preventing the flow predictions from effectively leveraging these sig-nals. Our proposed loss yields clear performance improvements, highlighting the benefit of explicit supervision on confidence and occlusion maps in addition to flow. 

Transfer Learning. We initialize compatible layers using pretrained weights from [47], originally trained for stereo depth estimation. This initialization sig-nificantly improves convergence behavior and leads to better final performance, demonstrating the effectiveness of transfer learning despite the task mismatch. 

Axis-wise Flow Refinement. We investigate two strategies for refining the optical flow components. In our default axis-wise refinement, the horizontal ( u)and vertical ( v ) components are refined independently, each with its own refine-ment head and update step as mentioned in Sec. 3.3. Beyond this point, additional iterations yield little benefit and can even slightly degrade performance. Balancing accuracy and computational cost, we adopt three refinement steps as the optimal configuration. 

## 5 Conclusion 

We presented FlowIt, a global matching network for optical flow that lever-ages optimal transport for robust initialization and confidence-aware refinement to propagate reliable motion cues into ambiguous regions. Rather than relying solely on local iterative updates, the model uses its own reliability estimates to selectively correct low-confidence predictions. This proves beneficial under occlu-sions and with transparent or reflective surfaces. Results across Sintel, KITTI, Spring, and LayeredFlow validate fine-tuned and zero-shot performance, while multiple model variants offer flexibility across computational budgets. Future work will focus on improving FlowIt capabilities to efficiently process high-resolution images, as well as extending it to process multiple consecutive frames and thus perform multi-frame optical flow estimation.
